Diagnosis
Why the Original Structure Was a Problem
This group can start a new page and still read as an intentional designed section.
01

Self-Competing Geo Campaigns

Running "Anxiety" in both the Ashburn and Falls Church campaigns meant bidding against yourself for the same searcher. Split budgets, split data, no way to see which copy or keyword actually drove results. Unified into one campaign with geo targeting handles this cleanly.

02

Zero Testing Coverage

The account had no ad groups targeting psychological testing, ADHD evaluations, gifted testing, kindergarten readiness, or autism assessments - services the practice offers. These represent an entirely uncaptured patient segment. The rebuild adds dedicated ad groups for all of them.

03

Intent Mixing

"Therapist near me", "therapist ashburn va", and "therapy" all in the same ad group means you can't match ad copy to search intent. A "near me" searcher is ready to book; a generic "therapy" searcher may just be researching. Separate intent layers let you write to each mindset.

04

Unstrategic Names & Structures

"Leads-Search-4", "Ashburn ad", "Ad group 1" - these names tell you nothing about what they target. When something breaks, you can't diagnose it. When performance is good, you can't replicate it. The new naming convention makes every ad group self-documenting.

Our Approach
How We Rebuilt This Account
Every decision in this rebuild came from data, not guesswork. We pulled the account's search term report and location performance report, classified every converting term, and used real CPA numbers to make structural and targeting decisions.
Phrase Match Only - No Broad Match
Every keyword in the rebuilt campaign uses phrase match exclusively. Broad match has no controls - Google will show your ad for any related search it decides is relevant, including competitors, out-of-area searches, and low-intent queries. Phrase match means "psychiatrist near me" only triggers when that phrase appears in the search, in that order.
The original account was spending on searches like "adhd coach near me" - a service THHL doesn't offer - likely because of broad match. That's wasted budget against the wrong patient intent.
Service-First Ad Group Naming
Every ad group follows the pattern: Service - Audience/Category - Intent Layer. For example: "Testing - ADHD - Local" or "Therapy - Anxiety - City". This means the ad group name tells you exactly what service it serves, who the audience is, and what kind of keyword intent it captures.
When you have 96 ad groups across 5 campaigns, you need to read a name and instantly know what it is. Vague names like "Anxiety" or "Ad group 1" collapse under that scale.
Intent Layers: General / Local / City / State
Every service gets four ad groups, one per intent layer. General = no geo modifier ("child psychiatry"). Local = proximity search ("child psychiatry near me"). City = explicit city ("child psychiatry ashburn"). State = state-level modifier ("child psychiatry virginia"). Each layer gets copy written for that specific intent.
"Near me" searchers are often ready to book now. "Virginia" searchers may be comparing providers across the state. They need different messages. One ad group for all of them wastes that difference.
Data-Driven Geo Targeting
Targeting is based on real CPA from the account's location performance report. Loudoun County converted at $137 CPA and Fairfax County at $271 CPA - both get +20% bid modifiers. Virginia statewide and Washington DC get +10%. Manassas showed zero conversions and gets a -25% reduction.
Treating every location the same wastes money on low-converting areas and underinvests in high-converting ones. We let the data decide, not assumptions.

RSA Asset Role Architecture
Each headline has an assigned role: keyword match, geo signal, credential, benefit, question, CTA, or proof. Each RSA gets 15 headlines covering all roles, so Google's machine learning can assemble high-relevance combinations for any searcher. Descriptions cover service fit, care process, location access, and CTA - one each, all four slots filled.
RSAs perform better when the asset pool gives Google meaningful variety to test. If all 15 headlines say the same thing differently, the system has no real options.
Build Now, Split Later
Campaign 1 starts with 9 ad groups in one campaign. As performance data accumulates, the highest-converting services and geographies get promoted into their own campaigns with dedicated budgets. The full taxonomy has 96 planned ad groups across 5 campaigns - all using the same naming and structure conventions.
Splitting too early fragments your data and makes optimization harder. Starting unified gives you a baseline, then you split where the data proves it's worth the budget isolation.

Naming System
Ad Group Naming Convention
Each name should explain service, audience, and intent without relying on internal notes.

Every ad group in the account follows this pattern. Read the name and you immediately know what it targets, who for, and what search intent it captures.

Service - Audience / Category - Intent Layer

Examples: Testing - ADHD - Local  |  Therapy - Anxiety - City  |  Psychiatry - Children - General

Intent Model
Intent Layers Explained
The same service can perform differently by general, local, city, or state intent.
Layer What it captures Example keywords Ad copy focus
General No geo modifier in the search query "child psychiatry", "adhd therapy" Service name + benefit + credential. Not geo-specific.
Local "near me" and close-proximity language "psychiatrist near me", "therapy near me" Geo-specific + proximity signal + CTA. Searcher is ready to book.
City Explicit city in the search query "therapist ashburn va", "psychiatrist falls church" City name in headline + service. Show you're specifically in that city.
State State modifier in the search query "psychiatrist northern virginia", "therapy virginia" State/region signal + in-person + telehealth. Wider reach.
